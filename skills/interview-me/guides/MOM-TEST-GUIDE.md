# The Mom Test — Avoiding Biased Feedback

> **Source:** The Mom Test by Rob Fitzpatrick (momtestbook.com) — how to talk to customers and learn if your business is a good idea when everyone is lying to you.

## Core Principle

People want to be helpful. They will tell you what they think you want to hear. The Mom Test is a framework for asking questions that get honest answers instead of polite lies.

## Three Types of Bad Data

| Type | Example | Why It's Bad |
|---|---|---|
| **Compliments** | "That's brilliant!" | Tells you nothing about whether they'd use it. Everyone is nice to your face. |
| **Future promises** | "I'd definitely pay for that." | People are terrible predictors. Ask about past behavior. |
| **Generic fluff** | "There's definitely a market for this." | Sounds positive, means nothing. No specific commitment. |

## Good Data vs Bad Data

| Bad | Good |
|---|---|
| "That's a great idea!" | "Last week we spent 3 hours manually doing X." |
| "I would use this." | "I already use a spreadsheet for this, but it breaks monthly." |
| "There's a need for this." | "I have $500 budgeted for this problem." |
| "The market is huge." | "My team of 8 has this exact problem right now." |

**The test:** If the user describes their problem with emotion and has built workarounds, it's real. If they're vague and polite, you're getting Mom Test responses.

## Question Transformation

1. **Deflect compliments** — "Thanks. What are you doing today for this?"
2. **Skip future-talk** — "Would you..." → "What have you tried?"
3. **Zoom to specifics** — "There's a need..." → "Who specifically? What happened last time?"

## Red Flags in User Responses

- Vague timeline: "someday", "eventually", "in the future"
- No specific person named: "people", "users", "the market"
- No past behavior: "I would", "I could", "I might"
- Generic enthusiasm without details
